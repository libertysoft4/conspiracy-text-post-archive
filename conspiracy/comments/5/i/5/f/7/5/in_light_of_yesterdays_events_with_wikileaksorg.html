I dont agree with you. If you connect to Tor from your home, the ISP knows you are using Tor. They may not know what youre doing, but they know youre using Tor. Using a VPN simply moves that trust to the VPN provider.

Lets say hypothetically Wikileaks has been taken over by the CIA and they are logging everyone that comes on. If you come in on Tor, it wont matter whether you connect to Tor from your ISP or from a VPN- they still have no idea who you are. Now lets say they drop some malware.

If youre going You--> ISP--> Tor--> Wikileaks, any malware defeating Tor will reveal your actual IP address assigned by the ISP.

If however youre going You--> ISP--> VPN--> Tor--> Wikileaks, any malware defeating Tor will reveal your VPN exit node IP (assuming they dont defeat OpenVPN, or that you have a firewall setup to block all packets not traveling the through VPN tunnel interface). In order to reveal you, theyd either need to get the VPN logs (which they may not have), or do timing correlation attacks to determine the entrance IP originating the requests leaving the VPN exit node that then went to the Tor entrance relay, etc.

Now, it could be useless. For all you know your VPN could be a honeypot or could easily be subdued by the .gov legally. Its impossible for us to know. Even in this worst case, its no worse than defeating Tor when you connect to it from your ISP.

As long as you have all leaks handled, Tor must be defeated to reveal your identity. They can use malware, or try to control the entrance and exit with honeypot nodes, but ultimately they must defeat it to get you (unless you reveal information related to other accounts with known links to your real IP etc).

A VPN is just another step in the chain. It could be anywhere from meaningless to a pain in their ass, but it certainly isnt worse. Ultimately, doing everything you can to ensure Tor isnt getting beat should be your priority.
