1  MrLumps 2017-11-10

Hey biology major and med student here. In essence that sounds like a good idea, but there can be a lot of problems, some deadly, of using bacteriophages as treatments. A few that come to mind are: - it’s kind of hard to make a virus that is so specific for a receptor or surface protein on bacteria’s surface, it involves a lot of DNA manipulation in order to create something that specific. Even tougher is that this artificial receptor you make on virus has to be JUST THE RIGHT AMOUNT of “sticky” for the bacteria target. Too sticky and the virus can easily mutate and become resistant, not sticky enough and the virus may start to accidentally target human cells or your gut microbiome, which is bad. - viruses have horrible DNA replication accuracy, which means they mutate ALOT (that’s why there’s a new flu vaccine every year and HIV is so hard to cure). So you spend all this time making a super complicated virus that binds to bacteria, but life finds a way, and bacteria have tons of ways to defend themselves. In the same way in which antibiotics are becoming obsolete, so would this virus. - shit’s expensive. It takes 10 years and billions of dollars to get a standard medication through the FDA. You have to have a big problem, whose cure would bring in big money to bring this virus to market. Not condoning current medical system but that’s way it currently is. - bacteriophages can only attack bacteria with cell membrane as outer layer, but a lot of super deadly bacteria have an additional capsule or tough outer layer which bacteriophages cannot penetrate (meningitis, strep) ALSO THIS IS CURRENTLY HAPPENING IN US BUT FOR CANCER. Last time I️ checked, duke was using viruses like this to cure Glioblastomas (very aggressive brain cancers). It makes more sense to use these viruses on cancers because cancers mutate a lot less than viruses, so don’t run into same problems.

Hope this explains why this isn’t particularly a conspiracy
