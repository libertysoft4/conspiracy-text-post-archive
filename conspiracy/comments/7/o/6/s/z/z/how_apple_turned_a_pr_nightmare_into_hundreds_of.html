I repair electronics for a living, I just want to clear up the first paragraph...

Apple didn't "make your batteries worse."

As they roll out new versions of iOS they understandably focus them on the newest, greatest phones and design them to use those phones to their fullest potential. Because the new versions of iOS run on older hardware though, they can strain them a bit.

Now, the issue is, the batteries inside your phone are Lithium based and degrade over time from charging and discharging over and over. When a battery degrades to a certain point, the phone can actually require more power while stressed than the battery can safely put out, so the phone shuts down. This is a pretty common thing on Android phones that have old batteries in them because of the fact that they didn't pull the shit that Apple did.

What Apple did is limit your processing power based on the health of your battery, so that your phone wouldn't demand more power than it could get from an old battery and shut down with no warning. They (wrongfully) decided that random shut-downs were a worse user experience than a slower processor.

I can't say I have a problem with their solution from a utilitarian perspective, but the way they went about it was definitely wrong. If they had been up front about what was going on from the get-go and/or just made their batteries easily replaceable or swappable this would be a really elegant solution. But instead, they charged $75 dollars for a battery replacement and continued to try and make it more difficult for third-parties to replace with every iteration of the phone.

In essence, don't necessarily get mad at the solution, the slowing down of the hardware, it was actually a pretty smart move, but feel free to hold against them the fact that they knew it was an issue an refused to make it easily or cheaply solvable and instead capitalized on it even more.

1  danwojciechowski 2018-01-04

Thanks for adding some corrections, AngryD09. I'll add a couple more things, also "as far as I know".

Owners of some older iPhones were experiencing "sudden, unexpected shutoff". The apparent reason was aging batteries. Most of these devices have a battery monitoring circuit which can, among other things, be used to safeguard the device against low battery voltage. This is apparently what was shutting down those older phones. It looks like Apple decided that reducing processor load, by either reducing processor speed or not using some cores, would help prevent the sudden shutdown scenario, so that's what they rolled out.

To answer some of your questions: Apple doesn't know when you have replaced your battery, but the battery monitor circuit no longer detects a weak/bad battery condition. The metric used to detect and assess how "worn-out" a battery is the drop in battery output voltage under load. All modern smart-phone processors have had the ability to run their processing cores at varying speeds, and to not use some cores for quite a while now. These features are used to enhance battery life by giving you processing power when you need it and saving power at other times. User friendly battery changing was sacrificed at the alter of sexy thinness. A user removable battery needs to be more robust, which usually means thicker, for safety, but super-thin is in, so we now have super thin batteries soldered into our phones. Last answer: once the battery has been replaced, the phone should operate at full speed again, until that battery wears out.

Finally, I totally agree with you that Apple should have been transparent about the update, and they should definitely given the user the choice of faster processor or unexpected shut-down (= slower processor) protection modes.
