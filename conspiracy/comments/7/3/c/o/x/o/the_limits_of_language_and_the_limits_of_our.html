Introduction

Our worldview is shaped by language, and our minds extend as far as language can extend. Language is both a freedom and a barrier, depending on how it is used. Language can be used to open minds or it can be used for control. Language is power.

This post will go in many directions, but the conspiracy is ultimately how language can be used for control. The only way to break out of this control, well, is to understand both the power and limitations of language.

First Things First

Before I write this post, I want to explain my views on language as an English teacher. This is extremely important, so I hope you do not bypass this part.

To me, there is no ‚Äúbad‚Äù language. Formal and informal language are necessary in different contexts. Slang, humor, and other aspects of language all hold a purpose. Language is not inherently good or bad. Language is a tool (I hold the same view with technology).

I tell my students that they have to learn various modes of language so they can function in different environments ‚Äì the way they would talk to the president is not the way they would talk to a friend. Further, it‚Äôs important to value one‚Äôs own home language (whatever that may be), but it is also important to learn and dissect the language of power. The more one is able to shift different modes of language, the more they can adapt to changing environments.

So, with that being said, I kind of laugh when I see grammar ‚ÄúNazis‚Äù go crazy on Reddit, a more informal message board. However, I could completely understand someone abiding by strict rules of grammar for their master‚Äôs thesis. It is also funny that many mock others for misuse of spelling or grammar, but they are often unaware of more miniscule mistakes they may make. For example, someone may criticize others for their misuse of ‚Äúits‚Äù and ‚Äúit‚Äôs‚Äù, but they may put a preposition at the end of a sentence, which is considered a ‚Äúno-no‚Äù in the grammar world. Did you know, for example, that many ‚Äòrules‚Äô of modern English come from Latin?

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_influence_in_English

The ‚Äònot ending a sentence with a preposition rule‚Äô is one of them (I‚Äôll explain why if anybody is interested). Latin was considered "pure" by early Linguistics. The point is, it‚Äôs really arbitrary (or is it?), and some of the rules of English can be absurd at times.

Still, the point is that formal language is not always necessary, and I do not believe anyone‚Äôs language is inherently good or bad. However, I believe that development of formal language is necessary for understanding the power structures in society, who ultimately use this formal structure of language. In reality, a person who truly understands language can shift in and out of different modes to suit the purpose of the situation.

Power Structures of Language

Now that I have explained that I do not think any language is inherently good or bad, I wanted to start with something that is really freaking me out.

‚ÄòThey‚Äô are now claiming that grammar is, well‚Ä¶racist:

http://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2017/feb/21/college-writing-center-proper-grammar-perpetuates-/

While it may be true that language is constantly evolving, I find it disturbing that standards of language are being compromised. Now, I do absolutely believe that, at times, language can be used as a system to oppress. There are many examples of this in history. However, we have two options here:

1) Help all people learn all aspects of language and all modes of language. For example, an oppressed person may not know English well. A good teacher can help this person learn the standards of language, and they can rise above.

2) Eliminate standards of English and downgrade it to a point where there is no oppression built in to any language.

Neither of these are perfect options. The first option presupposes that there is an elite group that will always be in control. My argument is that this elite will group will do whatever they can to limit the language of the ‚Äúcommon people,‚Äù and there will likely always be an elite group, whether we like that or not.

I am totally against number two though. By completely downgrading the standards of language, we will limit the worlds of the common folk.

I think we should fall somewhere in between. We should recognize how language can be used to control by those in power, but she not downgrade language to ‚Äúthe lowest common denominator‚Äù so to speak.

Before we go further, I need to explain something. All languages are built on systems ‚Äì semantics, syntax, phonology, grammar, etc. Every language system has these items. Further, ‚Äúgrammar‚Äù is not inherently good or bad either. It is a system to make communication more understandable and common amongst one group. No matter what language you explore, these common systems will exist.

If we remove systems of grammar, we start to remove clear systems of communication. It‚Äôs quite simple, and here is a classic example to show this:

I like cooking my family and my pets. I like cooking, my family, and my pets.

The simple comma rules saves us a lot of trouble in communication.

Further, when we remove a universal standard of a language, we remove those who have access to a universal understanding. For example, if most articles are written in standard English, then anybody familiar with standard English can understand them. More complicated subjects, like quantum physics, for example, cannot be ‚Äúbroken down‚Äù into simple language. Instead of doing that, we should teach all people the standard so they can access any topic.

If we start taking away rules of English, claiming they are inherently racist or bigoted, then we start breaking down something that unites us. No matter what race, ethnicity, class, etc., the majority in the United States are encouraged to learn standard English. What if that changes? Well, it seems to be‚Ä¶slowly.

So, while I cannot deny that power structures exist in any language system, I feel the benefit would be to provide all with access to the different levels of power versus denigrating a language system to meet the needs of the most limited speakers. In education, teachers should be encouraged to bring language and power to their students. If more are able to understand the language of those in power, then it is more likely that change can occur.

The question of transferability of literacy skills is in large measure related to how literacy is defined. The response is both affirmative and negative, and each will be considered in turn.

"Yes" Responses. Definitions which limit literacy to encoding/decoding skills and functional abilities generally involve rather low-level skills and are generally agreed to transfer. These include prereading skills of directionality, sequencing, ability to distinguish shapes and sounds, and knowledge that written symbols correspond to sounds and can be decoded in order and direction (Lessow-Hurley, 1990). Other skills include activation of semantic and syntactic knowledge; recognition of some rhetorical devices, and knowledge of text structure (Mace Matluck, 1982); learning to use cues to predict meaning (all languages are redundant) (Goodman, Goodman, & Flores, 1979); awareness of the variety of purposes for reading and writing (such as directing behavior--STOP!; providing information--ingredients of a food product, or news stories: describing a process such as how to set an alarm clock, and so on) (Hudelson, 1987); strategies, such as experimenting, hypothesizing, creating, constructing meaning; and perhaps most important, confidence in oneself as a reader and writer-especially important when the L1 is viewed negatively in the society (as with many Spanish speaking children in the U.S.) (Hudelson, 1987).

Evidence for the transfer of these kinds of literacy skills is abundant.

Nothing specific, just figured you might groove on the concept as an alternative to traditional grammar. Linguistics was my jam twenty-odd years ago; I'm rusty and not immersed enough to engage in a meaningful way on the subject anymore.

The mathematical representation aspect is what piqued my interest at first. Seemed so much more inherently obvious than the traditional grammar I was (disjointedly) taught in high school. I think the following excerpt speaks to that distinction.

From the wiki:

Chomsky distinguished between grammars that achieve descriptive adequacy and those that go further and achieved explanatory adequacy. A descriptively adequate grammar for a particular language defines the (infinite) set of grammatical sentences in that language; that is, it describes the language in its entirety. A grammar that achieves explanatory adequacy has the additional property that it gives an insight into the underlying linguistic structures in the human mind; that is, it does not merely describe the grammar of a language, but makes predictions about how linguistic knowledge is mentally represented.
