I'd like to discuss the movement from single-income to dual-income household, and the effect NOT having a homemaker has had on government revenue and GDP

162  2018-01-09 by drewshaver

Consider the following two situations:

Spouse A pulls in 100k/yr and it is enough to support the family, spouse B is a homemaker and does cooking, child rearing, cleaning, etc.

OR

Spouse A pulls in 100k/yr, and spouse B pulls in 60k/yr. In order to support the family, they have to spend 30k/yr on daycare, take-out / eating-out, a cleaning service, etc. After taxes, the marginal increase to discretionary spending is something like 10-15k/yr.

In the first scenario, the family pays income tax on 100k/yr, but day-to-day running of the family is mostly untaxed as the homemaker raises the children, makes dinner, cuts hair, cleans the house, and a dozen other things.

In the second scenario, the family pays income tax on 160k/yr, and pays sales tax on ~30k worth of eating out, haircuts, childcare, etc. In addition to that, all the people providing those services are each in turn paying income tax on their labor and corporate tax if the companies providing the services are profitable.

Moral of the story is, the movement towards a dual-income family model has made it so the government can tax the labor of many more adults. Before, the homemaker's labor was effectively untaxed -- as it wasn't being resold but kept inside the family. After, that labor became taxed, also became part of the GDP (which looks good for government?) and bolsters employment numbers (looks good on government reports).

In addition to this, there are all sorts of intangible benefits to having a homemaker, as it leads to a stronger nuclear family (not that it's impossible without, but it helps).

Intentional or not, this movement has drastically increased the revenue for income-taxing governments.
