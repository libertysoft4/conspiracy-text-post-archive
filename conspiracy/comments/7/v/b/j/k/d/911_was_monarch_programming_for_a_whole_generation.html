1  Vietoris 2018-02-05

It can measure the deviation from the zenith angle of two distant points.

Basically, a theodolite is a telescope where you can have an extremely precise measure of the angle between the line of sight of the telescope, and "the direction towards which objects are falling in the vicinity of the telescope" (I'm avoiding the word "down" because it is slightly misleading in this context). In other words, it's a telescope with a spirit level, but with a much better accuracy.

Now take two theodolites, and place them at a great distance from each other (typically, a few miles) but sufficiently close so that you can aim each one towards the other precisely (there are some crosshair inside the scope that allow you to do this with great accuracy).

If the Earth is flat, then the vertical directions are parallel and hence the sum of the two angles measured by each theodolite should be 180°. If the Earth is spherical, then the vertical direction are not parallel, and the sum of the two angles should be 180°+the angle corresponding to the section of Earth circumference between the two theodolites.

Of course, you should do many measurements, at various distances, and various heights, with various conditions to control the other variables, and reduce the margin of error.
