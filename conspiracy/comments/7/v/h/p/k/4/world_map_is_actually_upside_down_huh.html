1  Garagecouch 2018-02-05

What we view as being "north" or "south' is the direction that the sun falls or orbits.

This isn't correct. Maybe you've gotten confused by the variety of different "norths" people talk about.

North on Earth is toward the North Pole, being one of the two points where the earth's rotational axis intersects the surface. This is wholly arbitrary; the concept of "north" originates with the fact that north is to your left when you face the rising sun … approximately.

Solar north is the direction away from then sun which, when you look at the ecliptic from that direction, the earth is moving counter-clockwise. Solar north is vaguely in the same direction as polar north, but it's angled away by about 23°.

Galactic north is the direction away from the center of the galaxy which, when you look at the galaxy from that direction, the sun and the solar system are orbiting counter-clockwise. Galactic north is angled away from solar north by about 60°.

There is no meaningful correlation between galactic north and polar north. The only connection between the two is that the entire galaxy and all the matter in it has similar total angular momentum, originating far back in astronomical time when the galaxy was just a diffuse cloud of matter collapsing under its own weight. That cloud had a net angular momentum, and because angular momentum is conserved, the galaxy today still has that same angular momentum. This is why stuff orbits other stuff in the first place, rather than having long since collapsed into a big wad of matter with no rotation.
