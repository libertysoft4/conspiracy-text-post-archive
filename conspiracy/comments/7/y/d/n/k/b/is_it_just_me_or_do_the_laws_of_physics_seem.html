Like, when we look out at the universe we're seeing into the past, right? The speed of light is supposedly fixed, so when we're looking at, say, ursa minor, we're seeing it as it was 434 years ago. That seems... odd. Like, who knows what's happening just outside our vision!

We've been closely examining the sky for what, like 150 years? even less depending on your definition of "closely". What if the speed of light isn't in fact fixed, but is near infinite or infinite (a la newton)? What if instead of the speed of light being uniform in all the universe, we're in some kind of special bubble?

Like, look at this article from 1999: https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/1999/02/physicists-slow-speed-of-light/ . Scientists were able to slow photons to only 38mph! What if our entire solar system or even our entire region of space is enclosed in a bubble like that and our local speed of light is all slow? In that case, when we look at ursa minor we might very well be seeing it as it was 434 years ago, but if we looked from alpha centauri we'd see it as it was a couple microseconds ago.

It already seems that speed of light may have been faster in the past https://motherboard.vice.com/en_us/article/8q87gk/light-speed-slowed ...

So if the speed of light has been slowed down, especially in our local area of space, the question is... what's going on outside our local space that we're not supposed to see? Maybe the reason that we've never seen an extraterrestrial signal is simply because it's hidden behind a curtain of space-time! Maybe there's a whole galaxy out there and we're just kept in a snow globe unable to look out and see anything beyond weird smudges.
