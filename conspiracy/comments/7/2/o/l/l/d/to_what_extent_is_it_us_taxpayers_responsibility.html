Well that's the question, isn't it?

There are two basic points of view. One says that health is not guaranteed. Sometimes people get sick; sometimes they get really sick. That's just how it is. Medical care is not free. I don't mean it's not free-of-charge, I mean it's not free of cost. It takes resources (ingenuity and time) to provide medical care, which means it has to be paid for somehow. Doctors gotta eat, etc. Therefore it's an unfortunate fact that some people are more equipped to pay for medical care than others. Fundamentally this is no different from some people being more able to pay for nice clothes than others. We all need clothes, but you still gotta buy em. That's just life.

The opposing point of view starts with the premise that human suffering is to be minimized; that's a moral imperative. Therefore it's morally wrong to deny medical care to a person who's suffering solely and for no other reason than because that person cannot pay. Yes, medical care has a cost, but it's immoral to use that fact to separate people who are suffering from the help they need. Therefore we should establish some system — charity, subsidies, debt forgiveness, whatever — in which suffering people always have access to that which would alleviate their suffering.

There's no objective right or wrong on this. This isn't a question of fact. It's a question of choice. Do we choose to put medical care behind a money gate? Or do we choose to find some way of providing it to everybody regardless of ability to pay? Whichever side you come down on, there are a lot of practical details to work out. It's a problem nobody's ever fully solved.
