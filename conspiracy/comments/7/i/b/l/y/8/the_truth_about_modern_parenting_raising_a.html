The Truth About Modern Parenting: Raising a generation of Narcissists drowning not in their own reflections, but rather, in the emptiness of their screens.

164  2017-12-08 by polkadotgirl

How Kids are Raised Today

There’s a comic I came across a few years ago that I find highly relevant (give it a second to load):

https://www.google.com/search?q=teaching+comic+20+years+ago+vs+today&amp;tbm=isch&amp;tbo=u&amp;source=univ&amp;sa=X&amp;ved=0ahUKEwiXttTlkPnXAhUL9YMKHURxABIQsAQIKA&amp;biw=1366&amp;bih=652#imgrc=A89GplW-KEg5nM:

If you don’t feel like clicking, the premise is essentially that when students messed up in the past, the students themselves were blamed. Now, if a student messes up, the teacher is blamed.

Before I begin writing this post, I want to note a few things:

• I have a wonderful relationship with both parents and students

• I have very, very high ratings as a teacher in my state (I don’t care if you believe me, but I am actually being honored this month!)

• I am NOT saying all students or parents are like this – I am concerned that there are some clear growing trends that people are avoiding

• I work in a very low-income area, so my perceptions may be different than those working with students who come from higher socioeconomic backgrounds

• I am not a parent myself

I have to note those things because people will certainly avoid the main tenets of my post if I do not. Take my post as a generalization, and moreover, take it as a warning.

Yes, every generation complains about the upcoming generation, and I understand that will always be the case. But in this post, I want to propose that something different is happening. I feel the very structures of family and society are breaking (and technology is a huge culprit), but everything seems upside down.

Finally, I am not villainizing parents or even students; I am asking us all to reflect on larger society.

Parenting Styles

Every parent has their own belief about the right way to raise their child, but I first want to argue that parents do not even have time, energy, or even the money to think about the best way to raise their child. Unfortunately, there are some studies that show parents spend just 34 minutes a day with their children:

http://www.mirror.co.uk/news/uk-news/parents-spend-just-34-minutes-5518081

While studies may slightly vary, you have probably heard that children spend more time with their teachers than their parents, and I feel there is something really wrong there. Schools should NOT be raising children. When schools raise children, consequently, two things can happen:

1) With parents spending so little time with their children, the last thing they may want to do is discipline them. 2) Parents may leave the discipline (and other life essentials) up to teachers. Consequently, this can create a tense relationship between teachers and students, as they may be spending more time teaching Johnny how to behave than how to learn.

Parents may be over-worked and the last thing they have the time or energy to do is discipline their kids. So, there we see a problem in society. It is no secret that people are simply over-worked:

http://abcnews.go.com/US/story?id=93604

I feel for parents, but there are other issues at hand. On top of that, parenting styles have changed. While I do not advocate for extremes of the past, I also do not advocate for extremes of the present. Studies show that millennials are disciplining their children less:

https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/8b2b/9c4c1b15ccec6a49edac32de14f485f3e7c9.pdf

From the article,

“So whether they're opting for the "traditional" or harsh methods that were used to discipline them, or opting for alternatives and reinforcing positives, millennial parents are simply approaching discipline like they do every other aspect of their lives: in their own way.”

But that is where the problem lies. The lack of consistency in beliefs and morals in our society has created some chaos in the classroom. I can meet with 100 parents, and not one of them will agree on the “right” way to raise a child. To me, parents are doing what they think is best, but the problem is, nobody is actually trying to figure out what is best. For example, if disciplining “feels bad” or upsets the child, parents simply may not want to participate in it. While there will always be disagreements on the best way to raise a child, research is clear, authoritative parenting is best for children:

https://www.parentingscience.com/authoritative-parenting-style.html

To quickly note:

• Authoritarian parent: demand blind obedience from their children

• Permissive parent: reluctant to impose rules and standards

• Authoritative parent: warm, rational, high standards, negotiable

Which sounds like the best parent? Which sounds like the best teacher? We all hated the teacher who was so strict you could barely learn because of a fearful environment, and we all may have liked the teacher who let us do what we want, but we never really learned anything (and it did not benefit us in the end). It is clear, there needs to be a balance, and that balance seems to be lost.

Family Matters

Another huge issue is that more and more kids are being raised in single-parent homes, and while I am not here to villainize single parents, we need to be realistic. It’s simply just more difficult to raise kids on your own:

http://www.slate.com/articles/double_x/doublex/2012/07/single_motherhood_worse_for_children_.html

As a teacher, I am seeing more cases of neglect yearly, too:

https://www.huffingtonpost.com/2014/06/03/us-children-emotional-physical-abuse_n_5440277.html

Further, there is a serious problem in our society with lack of role models, especially male role models:

https://www.theguardian.com/society/2009/jan/19/children-earlyyearseducation

I do not care how controversial this is, but kids do better when they have both a mom and a dad. I am NOT saying that kids cannot fair well with just a mom and a dad, but statistically, they will do better. Studies show children fare better in traditional homes:

https://www.washingtontimes.com/news/2012/jun/10/study-children-fare-better-traditional-mom-dad-fam/

Despite the belief that gender roles do not exist or do not matter, there is something different that a child receives from a mother vs a father. I will tell you right now, that more than anything, my students do long for that ‘dad’ figure:

https://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/the-long-reach-childhood/201106/the-importance-fathers

From the article,

“There is no question that fathers do play an important part in their children's lives; that the majority of studies affirm that an involved father can play a crucial role particularly in the cognitive, behavioral and general health and well-being areas of a child's life; that having a positive male role model helps an adolescent boy develop positive gender-role characteristics; that adolescent girls are more likely to form positive opinions of men and are better able to relate to them when fathered by an involved father; that it is generally accepted, under most circumstances, a father's presence and involvement can be as crucial to a child's healthy development as is the mother's; and that experiencing validation of their importance in the general parenting literature has made fathers much more conscious of their value and, in turn, leads to their greater desire to be involved.”

Yet, daily, in mainstream media, men and fathers are villainized. There is this belief that these single women can do it all on their own, and while maybe they CAN, I would like to argue they should NOT.

As traditional family structures break down, our students lack a variety of role models, and they may seek those role models out in odd ways. Many children, for example, find celebrities to call ‘mom’ or ‘dad’.

http://www.mtv.com/news/2091316/friends-invented-calling-celebs-mom-dad/

Many of my students call me "mom" or "auntie", and while many of you may find that strange or abnormal, I understand it. Many go home and have a ‘mom’ but she may not be really there for them. Children seek mother and father figures, and they will seek guidance in those who even slightly resemble what should be their moms or dads. It breaks my heart.

Spoiled Kids

There, I said it, but what the actual…this one infuriates me. Look, I absolutely believe in providing for your children and giving them special things occasionally. But things are getting out of control.

Parents are spoiling their kids with ‘things’, not with love! Maybe in an effort to make up for being shoddy parents, many parents resort to materialism to get their kids to show their love. It’s not just material things, though, kids are literally ‘ruling the roost.’

https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2012/07/02/spoiled-rotten

From the article,

“Druckerman talked to a lot of French mothers, all of them svelte and most apparently well rested. She learned that the French believe ignoring children is good for them. “French parents don’t worry that they’re going to damage their kids by frustrating them,” she writes. “To the contrary, they think their kids will be damaged if they can’t cope with frustration.” One mother, Martine, tells Druckerman that she always waited five minutes before picking up her infant daughter when she cried. While Druckerman and Martine are talking, in Martine’s suburban home, the daughter, now three, is baking cupcakes by herself. Bean is roughly the same age, “but it wouldn’t have occurred to me to let her do a complicated task like this all on her own,” Druckerman observes. “I’d be supervising, and she’d be resisting my supervision.”

Also key, Druckerman discovered, is just saying non. In contrast to American parents, French parents, when they say it, actually mean it. They “view learning to cope with ‘no’ as a crucial step in a child’s evolution,” Druckerman writes. “It forces them to understand that there are other people in the world, with needs as powerful as their own.”

I have seen parents do everything they can to get their kids not to cry. Whatever happened to letting kids work through their emotions? They will get over it, right?

Yet, I wish you could see what some parents expect in a classroom. There is now a legal form for nearly everything a kid might experience. While I am certainly happy there are legal protections for students who need them, this system is abused. There is one student I have who gets occasional headaches, and they can leave class whenever they want according to this form. It certainly isn’t surprising they leave when the work becomes a little tedious. Still, I won’t dismiss that maybe they really do have a condition and need a break (and I’m not against breaks!), but I wonder if parents have ever questioned if the student was lying. I don’t know at this point, and I don’t really care. I do my job, and I listen to the forms. Still, the future may be harder for these kids because the real world is NOT like that. When I get a headache at work, I have to keep working.

These students start to believe that this is how the real world operates, and this sets them up for failure ultimately.

To me, kids are spoiled, but it isn’t their fault. Of course, parents want what is best for their children, so they believe that they must give into every whim for their child to be successful.

However, in my experience, the most successful students have parents who call out their BS, even when they do not like it. This does NOT mean they are screaming at their kids or abusing them or anything like that. They just are not going to play games.

Sensitivity or Self-Centeredness?

We have ALL heard the words “safe space” and “triggers.” While I admire that this generation (and the upcoming one) may be more sensitive, I actually believe this sensitivity is simply a form of self-centeredness. Look, I am not saying that ‘triggers’ and ‘trauma’ do not exist (I am an abuse victim myself), and there is good reason for appropriate sensitivity:

https://www.theguardian.com/higher-education-network/2016/mar/10/student-protests-are-young-people-too-sensitive-these-days

Still, there is a point where we are teaching students that the world is always going to cater to their individual needs, and to me that is dangerous. Even if these college campuses do this, the real world simply is not like that.

And while students may be more centered on their own needs, they certainly are not worried about anybody else around them, and that is where the problem lies! Sensitivity is fine, but when one expects the world to revolve around them, and they do not even consider the world…well, that is the type of student we are raising.

It is no surprise that students are less empathetic than ever:

https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/what-me-care/

From the article,

“But the new finding that empathy is on the decline indicates that even when a trait is hardwired, social context can exert a profound effect, changing even our most basic emotional responses. Precisely what is sapping young people of their natural impulse to feel for others remains mysterious, however, because scientists cannot design a study to evaluate changes that occurred in the past. As Twenge puts it, “you can’t randomly assign people to a generation.”

There are theories, however. Konrath cites the increase in social isolation, which has coincided with the drop in empathy.”

So, sure, maybe kids are more aware of themselves than ever, but they certainly do not seem to care about anyone but themselves (unless it impacts them). They are drowning themselves in their own reflections. : )

Technology

Not only are we more isolated socially, but technology is creating the illusion that we more are connected:

http://highexistence.com/how-technology-has-created-the-illusion-of-socialization/

I have written SO much about technology that I am going to summarize my main points about it here. I

1) Technology is just a tool. It is not inherently good or bad. It is how it used.

2) The problem is that 50% of teenagers feel they are addicted to their phones. Then, it is no longer a tool. It is a problem:

http://www.cnn.com/2016/05/03/health/teens-cell-phone-addiction-parents/index.html

3) The problem is that I work with students who would rather be on their phones then play basketball, learn, or hang out with friends (sitting on phones in a room of people does NOT count as hanging out with friends).

4) Technology is increasing narcissism, which goes back to the sensitivity vs. self-centeredness point:

https://www.theatlantic.com/health/archive/2013/03/the-internet-narcissism-epidemic/274336/

5) Technology is making people less empathetic:

https://www.luc.edu/media/lucedu/law/centers/childlaw/childed/pdfs/2014studentpapers/Bonnette.pdf

And ultimately, technology is replacing parenting. Technology is the new “pacifier” that calms down children:

https://www.forbes.com/forbes/welcome/?toURL=https://www.forbes.com/sites/jordanshapiro/2013/06/11/national-study-mobile-devices-are-changing-parenting-childhood-and-family-values/&amp;refURL=https://www.google.com/&amp;referrer=https://www.google.com/

Look, I use technology daily in my classroom, and in many ways, it has opened up the world to my students. The problem is, at the end of the day, kids are NOT using technology to learn about things they are interested in. They are using it to be entertained.

Many are now living their lives through a screen, where everything can be “filtered”, even reality. In fact, I feel that students are becoming more disconnected with reality because it is so easy to filter anything with technology. Living through a screen is not the same as living.

Earlier I was talking to a friend, and he said, if he ever wanted to rule the world, he would simply use technology to create endless distractions and entertainment. I think he’s on to something…

Anything Goes! Everybody is SPECIAL! You CAN BE ANYTHING!!!

First and foremost, we live in a society where there truly is a degradation of standards or morals. Almost anything goes now, and this is viewed as “progressive.” If you think the guy who identifies as a “transracial Filipino” then YOU are a bigot, and there is something wrong with YOU, not society:

https://nypost.com/2017/11/13/transracial-man-was-born-white-identifies-as-filipino/

http://www.wnd.com/2016/09/child-4-worlds-youngest-ever-sex-change-patient/

We are treating children as wise – not innocent. We, adults, are losing our wisdom.

We tell kids that they can be anything and they will always be catered to, and we are lying to them! More and more college students are struggling to find jobs, but we keep telling kids the world is theirs:

https://www.cnbc.com/2016/12/15/why-college-grads-cant-find-jobs-commentary.html

Look, I’m not saying we have to bring children down, but I feel like we are allowing them to live in a fantasy in order to cope with reality. On top of lack of discipline and standards and empathy, we are not letting our kids see reality for what it is. It’s not that we shouldn’t push kids toward their dreams. The problem is, I think we are letting kids live as if the world is a dream.

What about Education?

Education in itself is a conspiracy, and I have written so much about this. Still, I wanted to summarize a few points about education that are contributing to severe problems in our society. I wanted to focus more on education related to parenting, than education itself (as that would require a doctorate thesis right now):

1) Kids are not allowed to be kids anymore. Playgrounds are gone. Parents are suing kids like crazy. Anything is a law suit, the ‘Red Rover’ law suit:

https://www.nbcsandiego.com/news/local/Santee-School-District-Settles-15K-Red-Rover-Lawsuit-with-Parents-230152241.html

2) Kids are coming too school knowing too much about things they cannot understand. Teachers are expected to engage kids who are interested in adult themes, yet may not even have reading skills. This creates a HUGE issue. For example, a kid may have a grade 3 reading level, but they may already be watching anything they want about any topic at home. Traditional books to teach them how to read just are not going to interest them. (I could write a whole other post about this). I mean, Curious George is not interesting when you are five years old and already know what sex is...

3) Parents and teachers have become enemies in many ways. Divide and conquer?:

https://www.theguardian.com/teacher-network/teacher-blog/2014/nov/20/private-school-parents-teachers

4) Kids do not know how to ‘be bored’ anymore. I tell my students, “Sometimes things are boring, and that’s okay.” Boredom can allow creativity and ideas, yet students may not even know how to cope with boredom:

http://community.today.com/parentingteam/post/the-im-bored-generation-heres-why-our-kids-need-to-be-unplugged-and-restarted_1490029767

5) In schools, children are treated as numbers. They are often no more than a test score. They are treated as robots who must perform. We all know the dangers of testing mania in our country. Are tests necessary? Absolutely! Is there a profit behind this all? Um…yeah:

https://www.huffingtonpost.com/rainesford-alexandra/the-business-of-standardi_b_9785988.html

Schools have become businesses, but they are NOT businesses (or they should not be).

I’m willing to answer any questions about education that I have not addressed here.

Conclusion

We are failing our kids. They look to us, adults, but all they see us doing is posting selfies and statuses on social media.

Still, it goes beyond that. The conspiracy is that this is ALL purposeful. I believe that our children are being dehumanized. They are living in a world of technology where they can become Gods themselves (or at least think they can).

Nobody is reflecting - except on the screen in front of them. The modern Narcissist from the classical myth would die in the blackness of his screen, maybe not in a pool of water.

To end with my favorite quotes about parenting:

“It is easier to build strong children than to repair broken men.” – Frederick Douglass

“Don’t worry that children never listen to. Worry that they are always watching you.” – Robert Fulghum

“Everybody knows how to raise children, except the people who have them.” - P.J. O'Rourke

I ended with that quote because I know that raising children is NOT easy, and I do not have my own child. Still, years of teaching hundreds upon hundreds of students has given me some insight.

The best parents, I have seen, expect the best for their children, within the confines of reality, and they prepare them for the real world, with warmth, love, and dignity.
