1  pestacyde 2017-11-07

In 1997, my then husband and I knew we wanted out of the rat race before we even really knew what the race was all about. We bought 5 acres in a small town far away from the cities and put a modular home on it.

A year or two after living there, along came Y2K. Being young and nieve at the time, I bought into it hook line and sinker. Hell, we had JUST got a home computer and now the world was gonna end!? I began devouring anything I could read on homesteading and off the grid living before there was even a kitschy term for it.

By New Years day 2000, we had aquired another 9 acres around us, another couple in their own home sharing the property, and were for all intents and purposes self sufficent with 1 child of our own and the neighbors having 2.

Well, Y2K came and went. Cue me becoming the butt of everyones jokes. Sure, I was glad that the world hadn't come to a standing halt, but I despised being dumb enough to get sucked in. Then it dawned on me, I may have jumped the gun on survive or die, but I was still WAY better off than most everyone I knew. I was 24 years old and had gained a life time of experience that I, as a city girl, would have otherwise never learned.

I still own the property, the houses are gone but not the friendships and I've since moved to a subburb. But I can never unlearn what Y2K taught me so I decided to give that back to society. I bend anyones ear that will listen about living self sustainabiliy and I've spent years volunteering in my community. Not only to share what I learned, but to forge ties with those people so that should the world collapse one day, I know who my friends are.

I may not be out there as an activist, demanding change and answers these days but I feel like I've got survival on lockdown and should the day ever come, unless it's happening in my front yard, me and mine will be ok.
