1  OperationMobocracy 2018-08-20

I would guess there were limits on weapons seen as primarily martial in nature, but many of these weren't practical to own anyway because of their cost of creation and limited utility.

But through a lot of history, armies weren't professional fighting forces armed by a central authority. They were raised by local nobles who pressed the peasants into service and they were expected to bring their own weapons.

There's also a blurry line between a military weapon and a tool for utility. A short axe or hatchet was a battlefield staple for a long time, plus it has all kinds of other uses. And none of that includes improvised weapons that combine the sharp bit of a tool meant for one thing combined with a different shaft or handle. Put that knife on the end of a stick and it's now a spear.

My guess is that prohibitions on non-gun weapons ownership was mostly a later phenomenon when an area wasn't threatened by invasion and the local nobility wanted to keep a lid on insurrection and had the funds to pay for a full-time standing force to deal with whatever problems cropped up.

It was probably also more popular in urban areas partly as a means for keeping order. The fewer fighting weapons the peasants had, the less likely they were to rebel OR get into tribal-type conflicts among themselves.
