The problem with that is it's already legal standard that IPs aren't bulletproof ways to prove identity. Any ISP on the planet using IPv4 is using dynamic IP addressing for residential users, and they frequently to infrequently change the IP assigned to a specific customer. There is no requirement to keep IP logs for any amount of time in the US for ISPs.

This means if Asshole Conglomerate decides to use Shady As Fuck LLC to track torrent users, but Good Guy ISP doesn't keep logs outside the current user it's entirely possible Good Guy ISP will be forced by a court to hand over the identity of Fuckwad McPantyMoister instead of Scumfuck Tinklebottom that actually pirated the file.

This advances the lawsuit against Mrs. McPantyMoister, which she will agree to settle or spend several thousands of dollars she doesn't have defending against, when in reality Mr. Tinklebottom was the one pirating and neither individual had any idea or consent to the IP change.

You'd think that this is rare, but it's not. There are dozens of cases of companies like Shady As Fuck LLC randomly suing people based solely on IP pull requests of ISPs, that are then later dropped when either the company simply did not receive enough data from the ISP to prove a Mrs. McPantyMoister actually downloaded a file or not, or because Mrs. McPantyMoister can objectively prove they did not subscribe to the ISP the Shady As Fuck LLC alleges.

Hmm, immediate Googling says IP addresses can be used in criminal cases.

It can be used for search warrants to get better evidence, but an IP address itself is incredibly weak evidence as it is at best a circumstantial link. It identifies that the Route for Subscriber X with ISP Y exists and was connected to Illicit content Z. It inherently cannot prove identity, as it is the address of the router, not an individual computer (in 99% of modern cases), and thus no single individual is inherently responsible until it is proven additionally (via Computer forensics). If all someone has is an IP and Time, the case will likely be thrown out.

Some decent arguments around it are here: https://www.expertlaw.com/forums/showthread.php?t=94352

I'm pretty anti-piracy except for defunct companies where the copyright is likely in a grey area or otherwise unenforceable, as it's typically the only way to get the content at all.
