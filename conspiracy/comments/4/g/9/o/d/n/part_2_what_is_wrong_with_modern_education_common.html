I want to note that I have nothing against standards in education for teachers, students, or administrators (even parents!), but there is something deeper here.

Who really wrote the CCSS?

According to the CCSS official website, teachers were involved in the creation of these standards. Why aren’t specific names/credentials/etc. listed on the CCSS site? It is extremely vague. I found one site that claims that in reality, there were few teachers on the CCSS committee from https://dianeravitch.net/2014/04/28/mercedes-schneider-who-are-the-24-people-who-wrote-the-common-core-standards/

She writes, "Altogether, 24 people wrote the Common Core standards. None identified himself or herself as a classroom teacher, although a few had taught in the past (not the recent past). The largest contingent on the work groups were representatives of the testing industry."

Even if that is not a concern to you, let’s take a deeper look. Jason Zimba, one of the creators, pushes that “The Common Core would drive publishers and test-makers to create better curricula and better tests and push school districts and teachers to aim for excellence, not just basic proficiency, for their students. And the guidelines would arm every principal, teacher and parent with the knowledge of exactly what it takes to get into college and succeed.” http://www.npr.org/sections/ed/2014/12/29/371918272/the-man-behind-common-core-math

What does that even mean? If you really look at that, it is a broad generalization with A LOT of implications. Why is proficiency a bad thing now? I am not against academic 'excellence', but I am when there is a larger agenda.

So, what is wrong with the CCSS?

Here is a pretty good article that sums up some of the problems:

http://www.tfpstudentaction.org/politically-incorrect/common-core/10-reasons-why-common-core-is-bad-for-education.html

I’m going to focus on some of the areas that stand out to me as an ELA teacher first.

•Move toward largely informational texts: The main argument is that students will be living in a world where informational texts will be more valued than ever. If that is true, then students should spend even more time with literary analysis. Not only do literary texts help students understand controversial issues, but they require higher order thinking skills that are not always present when reading informational texts. Basically, by 6th grade, we are pushed to teach half informational/half literally analysis. By high school, students are largely only supposed to be reading informational texts. In addition, these informational texts are often biased and clearly fit a certain agenda. I could probably share actual articles that are pushed for me to teach, but I don’t know if it would be smart to do that.

•Text-based evidence/No more critical thinking: There is seriously no more room for creative thinking. Do not get me wrong. I understand the value of students citing evidence, proving what they say, and making inferences from a text. Those are important skills, but that is all you do in English classes now. Creative writing has gone out the door. In fact, there is absolutely nearly no room for opinion-related writing, persuasive writing, or critical thinking outside the text. There is a level of rigor to this, but students are finding it increasingly more difficult to think for themselves.

*Middle school classic example of an informational/argumentative essay: Develop a thesis to support a claim on a controversial topic. Be sure to include credible research for your position as well as a counter-argument.

*CCSS modern writing prompt: The articles you just read make a claim that zoos are good for animals. Write an essay on the types of evidence the authors use. Cite evidence from the text to support your essay. (This is really an essay I taught this year).

Clearly, I vary my writing curriculum even if my standards do not allow me, too, but there is that pressure for my students to perform.

What do you think about the difference? I could give more examples, of course.

•Testing, testing, testing: This is endless. This is probably what you hear about the most. The more tests they give, the more money huge corporations make.

https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/answer-sheet/wp/2015/03/30/report-big-education-firms-spend-millions-lobbying-for-pro-testing-policies/

I’m not anti-testing, but I’m anti-testing for corporate agendas to make money off of children.

I can definitely get deeper into this, but the tests themselves are unfair to children of all backgrounds. New York is a great example of what is wrong with these tests:

http://www.nytimes.com/2015/08/13/nyregion/new-york-state-students-standardized-tests.html?_r=0

•Math mania: I don’t teach math, but I would never want to under the CCSS. There are so many problems with the math CCSS, I honestly think that if you said you supported them, I would automatically think you were a shill (oops, I didn't mean to include my opinion here!). I see thousands of articles like these:

http://thefederalist.com/2015/09/21/frustrated-dad-shows-why-common-core-math-is-awful/

http://www.techinsider.io/insane-common-core-math-problems-go-viral-2015-10

The greatest argument for CCSS is it helps students understand ‘why’ or ‘how’ the formulas work. In reality, I believe an overly complex, one-size fits all approach to teaching math undermines children’s development. This is well-stated:

“It's an odd pedagogical agenda, based on a belief that conceptual understanding must come before practical skills can be mastered. As this thinking goes, students must be able to explain the "why" of a procedure. Otherwise, solving a math problem becomes a "mere calculation" and the student is viewed as not having true understanding.

This approach not only complicates the simplest of math problems; it also leads to delays. Under the Common Core Standards, students will not learn traditional methods of adding and subtracting double and triple digit numbers until fourth grade. (Currently, most schools teach these skills two years earlier.) The standard method for two and three digit multiplication is delayed until fifth grade; the standard method for long division until sixth. In the meantime, the students learn alternative strategies that are far less efficient, but that presumably help them "understand" the conceptual underpinnings.”

http://www.theatlantic.com/national/archive/2012/11/a-new-kind-of-problem-the-common-core-math-standards/265444/

If you search problems with CCSS math, there are thousands of articles. The biggest problem I see is that students are being told it is wrong to have any rote memorization in math. Students that are far behind in math or falling even further behind. Math, a subject often notoriously hated by students, may not be in perfect shape, but the standards are NOT helping. When I do occasionally try to help math students with their homework, I am told my way of helping them is wrong, even though I will arrive at the same conclusion as them. What are they doing to our math curriculum and why?

•CCSS targets poor schools: The CCSS were supposed to create equity in standards across the country, but that is a complete myth. This article does a really good job of explaining that:

https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/answer-sheet/wp/2014/03/10/the-myth-of-common-core-equity/

As a teacher working at a title 1, poor school, the CCSS have not benefited my students, changed test scores, or showed changes in equity of my students. Test scores have stayed the same though we are working students harder. If there is a rise in test scores, it’s because we have been teaching to the test to the best of our abilities. I train my students for the type of writing prompts they will come across on the test. You can judge me, but my evaluation depends on my students’ scores.

While I do admit that there are some lessons that are my rigorous, the students who cannot keep up are falling further and further behind. The only thing I can do is ‘secretly’ teach a different curriculum to students who are not at the level they are supposed to be.

No Child Left Behind (NCLB) is still in place. Poor schools are still getting punished for poor test scores.

I could even argue the ELA standards are not that bad, but in combination with a testing agenda, they certainly are detrimental to students.

•The ultimate move toward privatization of education: A simple google search will yield thousands of results about the goals of privatization of education across the board, and I believe the CCSS is just one of the many ways this goal will be achieved. Even if they were to completely get rid of CCSS, something new will come along, all with the goal of standardizing education from a government/corporate force. There are many reasons why privatizing education is bad, but I enjoyed this blog:

http://nepc.colorado.edu/blog/why-education-must-be-public-not-privatized

Conclusion: I could go on and on as always, but the CCSS is just one of the problems in modern education, in a movement toward privatization where corporate interests control what our students are learning. Not only is critical thinking becoming a skill of the past (unless you count CCSS math?!?!?…), but the curriculum is more and more controlled, with test scores still being used as the tool for how well schools perform, when in reality, they show little more than how many students are impoverished and come from difficult backgrounds.

Who is going to do good on my state tests in a few weeks? I could print out a spreadsheet and mark my students with those that have involved parents, and those who do not. I could then mark the students who are poor and who are “rich”, and then I could compile all the data, and I guarantee the students from sound economic backgrounds with parent involvement will perform well, while the others will not. That's what studies show!

While there are many flaws in our modern education system, what is always overlooked is parent involvement. Parent involvement trumps class when it comes to achievement. There are thousands of articles on this, but I know it for a fact as a teacher. Though anecdotal evidence is often discounted, the students who have parents who can/will be involved care and perform better. Does our society create an environment for parents to actually be involved? Not when they are working full-time, often with single parent homes involved. Plus, I don't know how many parents come to me about CCSS math, wanting to help their students but just not understanding how. We need to create parent friendly schools - not schools where students are raised by the teachers and the government.

It’s not that I know if there is much we can do, but I am finding the general population less and less aware in one of the arguably most important aspects of our society: education!

I do not forsee a lot of good coming out of the future of education. I do not mean to be a pessimist, but I think the best I can do is write on these topics and hope that they touch people here and there.

I think one of the biggest arguments I'll get on this thread is that I fear change/rigor in education, but that is just not true. I have no issue having my 6th graders write an essay per week. What I do take issue with is we are creating students with certain skills that one might claim are 'better' and lead students toward' excellence', but in reality, these students will not be able to find a good career path for them.
