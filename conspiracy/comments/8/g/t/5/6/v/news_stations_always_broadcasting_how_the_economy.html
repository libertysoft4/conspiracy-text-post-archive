1  WestCoastHippy 2018-05-03

SF Bay Area. Tent cities all over the place in SF and Oakland. I pass several, 2-8, per daily commute. I pass a city-run "tiny home" lot with around 30 tiny homes, surrounded by barbed wire and flood lights, to house the homeless. I see tent cities with painted fences, flags, and signage ("12th St Commons" or "Camp Ne'er Do Well" or "Home Street Home" for example). Those are organized and have leadership/infrastructure from within. Virtually every underpass is edge to edge tents. Its strikingly weird when an underpass is cleared (and if so it means that underpass is used by pedestrians). Some camps are spilling into the shoulders of roads, so there is no curb or other boundary between traffic and tent (I assume these are storage tents). Virtually every patch of non-paved ground is taken. Lil grass cut-outs between freeway off-ramps: camp. That circle in the middle of the big round on-ramps: camp. The strip of grass between off-ramp and industrial zone: camp. Any small city park: camp.

There is a big ole city lot, empty, surrounded by freeway, a church, and industry. There is a service entrance/maintenance access passage in the sound wall between freeway and neighborhood. Thru this hole lives, I dunno, dozens. They have fires every night and there is at least 2 cars parked here all the time. Sometimes up to 8-10 cars. I believe the people are working as cars are gone mid-day, lots at night/weekends.

There are 2-5 RV and car campers on my block (they rotate around), so there is a whole other strata of homeless not in tents. In industrial zones, the RV becomes the center of a camp, and they'll use the RV and nearby fencing to tie-up tarps, secure pallets-as-walls, and otherwise set up infrastructure. One RV-centered camp has the RV, and then two pallet homes. They take pallets, raft them together, attach wheels to the bottom, then build simple wooden boxes on the pallet-raft, complete with simple door and lock. These are maybe 8' x 10' x 10'. The wheels keep it over any puddle, and if the cops come, they can wheel the unit to a new spot.

It's nuts.
