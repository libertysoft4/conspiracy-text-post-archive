I think that's mostly what I'm trying to say.

Have you ever heard of E-Prime? It's a form of English which doesn't use any form of the verb to be. So you can't say "Bob is a normie," you'd have to phrase it "Bob is acting like a normie now," or "Bob often deals with new information like a normie."

While acting like a normie might be part of Bob's behavior, it's not a part of his identity, as saying he is a normie would imply. If Bob stops acting like a normie today and starts acting like a seeker, then his behavior would change and my description of his behavior would change. But Bob is still Bob; his identity did not change.

I believe this is an important distinction because the language we use to describe our reality affect the way we perceive and act in the world. When we say Bob is a normie, we've already set up a situation in which we feel our identity, our ego, is superior to Bob's identity, and that will color the interaction.
