1  CDeMichiei 2018-06-27

I think using the term Unobtanium is causing the confusion. I'm assuming that your overall hypothesis is that something will be mined from landfills in the future... Something different from modern day oil deposits. We can tell now that, over time, potentially valuable hydrocarbon deposits will form within old landfills. The unique mixture of elements may effect the overall composition of the hydrocarbon deposits, so your hypothesis can't be ruled out completely.

However, when it comes to metals/heavier elements, the environment required to create new chemistry simply isn't available on Earth. The heavier elements will remain in their current form, or slowly decay into a more stable isotope. In order to manipulate atoms of that size, you need something like a star, or even better: a supernova.

In short, if anything new or unique comes out of modern landfills, it will consist of the carbon-based building blocks we already have available to us. There's really no possibility of new elements forming, or heavier elements changing (minus half-life decay).

1  Ikonovich 2018-06-27

The metallic elements will tend to stay in the exact form they enter the landfill except for oxidation, which in covered landfills should occur at a relatively slow pace and which occurs at a different rate for all materials - Iron for example oxidizes fairly quickly, whereas aluminum oxidation forms a thin layer of aluminum oxide that protects the underlying aluminum from oxidation.

Simple organic materials are broken down by anaerobic bacteria which is why some landfills are used to source methane.

More complex organic materials, specifically plastics, actually have to wait for evolution; some plastic-eating bacteria have been discovered, but for the large part locked deep in a landfill these materials have nothing to degrade them and may be recognizable in 100,000 years.

The only thing that could change all this would be getting subsumed by the earth due to geological activity, at which point the metals would eventually work deep enough (and therefore hot enough) to be combined with other materials forming raw ores. All materials not metallic or concrete will at this point be reduced to basic elements. This process takes millions of years, however.
