Facebook has increasingly been a target lately. But why Facebook and not other social media sites guilty of the same disregard for privacy? What makes Facebook unique? I've got a theory: its powerful capacity for grassroots organization.

50  2018-03-27 by dancing-turtle

In all the recent controversy about Facebook, this is something I've seen discussed very little. The revelations about Cambridge Analytica were hardly shocking to those of us who've long been calling out the risks of giving out personal data like Halloween candy to all these third-party apps. It's good to see people wising up, but you've got to wonder, why now? And why Facebook specifically?

"Fake news", echo chambers, data-mining, privacy breaches, and other problems with social media are common to practically all platforms. But one of the main reasons many of us are reluctant to give up Facebook entirely is its incredible usefulness and broad reach for organizing events -- everything from private parties to public protests. No other social media site even comes close to Facebook in terms of event planning and organizing. Unless something else fills that gap and attracts similar numbers, decimating Facebook's userbase -- and even just making users more wary of political engagement on Facebook -- would greatly diminish public activism against injustice, war, authoritarian overreach, etc.

I hesitate to defend Facebook, because the platform does have a ton of problems. But I think it's important that we critically analyze why Facebook is being singled out from the crowd, and why now. If nothing else, maybe we can take measures to ensure that public activism isn't a casualty of taking a stand for digital privacy.
