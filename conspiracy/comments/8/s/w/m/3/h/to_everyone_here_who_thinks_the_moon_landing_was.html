Hi, im a dude who works with cameras and lights a lot.

I also work with the sun, which is a fickle and bouncy, yes bouncy beast.

First off, I won't deny that there is a non-zero chance of the Moon landing being faked. Or at least one of them. It just would have been insanely hard in 1968. (Technically earlier than that, as there were earlier manned flights that didn't land on the moon, as well as ones in low Earth orbit)

To the "two light" conspiracy. Sunlight can reflect off of lighter colored surfaces and illuminate shadowed areas. As a lighting technician for movies (aka the kind of guy who would be called to fake a moon landing with movie lights) I see sunlight "bounce" (thats the industry term) off of plenty of surfaces. Including things like gravel and sand. We actually take advantage of large, reflective surfaces a lot in movie lighting, because bouncing light can create very pleasing soft shadows, and a more flattering look on the talent. One thing we sometimes do if we have no choice but to film under ugly high noon light outdoors is to lay out white reflective fabric under the actors to fill in dark, shadowed eyes and stuff. Its actually pretty similar to what happens on the moon. You can think of the entire lunar surface as one big bounce card. So even though the sun creates deep, black shadows on the moon (especially in absence of an atmosphere), the reflective nature of the lunar surface can actually fill in some of those shadows, depending on the angle of the sun. Also consider the fact that the astronauts are wearing white, and therefore will stand out better in shaded areas.

The nail in the coffin for me though about the double light theory is... take a look at the shadows. You only see one sharp shadow per object. Part of the nature of light is that the bigger the light source, the softer and less defined of a shadow. The smaller, the sharper. We only see one sharp shadow being made in all of the photos and video of any given object on the moon... that'd be the sun. If you look at the sun (please use proper eye wear) from our perspective on earth or the moon, it looks quite small because its very far away. You can blot it out with your thumb. So a small point-source, relative to objects on the moon, makes a very sharp and defined shadow. If we saw two shadows of the same quality and same relative brightness (or lack thereof) FAKE BRO! But we don't.

What we do see are much bigger and softer shadows created by the reflectivity of the lunar surface. End only in areas that are shaded from the sun (or "key" light source if you'd like)

And if by some miracle you do catch two sharp shadows on the moon of the same object... what else is up there but a movie lig....oh wait that might be the Earth. Yup, the earth reflects a little light back onto the Moon as well, depending on how much of the surface is illuminated. Its part of why you can see the moon partially lit in the daytime when conditions are right.

Another nail in the coffin on this one, look at their visors. You only see one light source reflected, and you never EVER catch a camera reflection. This used to be a giant problem for movies back in the day, before CGI. We would and still do try and cover equipment with tarps and things, but if you look close in windows, sunglasses, etc. in old movies, you can always see movie gear and crew members.

Nowadays you can digitally paint out reflections, but in 1968, that technology did not exist. Its like saying "yeah no way did we have the tech to go to the moon, but we did somehow manage to remove all the inevitable camera, light, and crew member reflections from their visors in post production". That would have required a level of computer control, even if you could find a method to do it on film prints with a robotically controlled exacto-knife or something, that would have been far in excess of computer "alleged" to be flown on Apollo or used on the ground at that time. Even later optical printing devices used for the Starwars films in the 1980's had computer control in excess of what existed on earth in the 1960's.

And for the inevitable "but they could have used big movie bounce material in addition to one light" I urge you again, look at their visors. The next time you watch a movie with someone wearing any type of glasses, or even just a closeup where you can see eyeballs, look at the reflections. 9/10 you'll see a big white square, trapezoid or something with sharp edges. Thats a bounce!

Had the moon landings been faked with more than one light source, they would have been all over the visor reflections. And, I argue, so would the cameras, rigging, light stands, and Stanley Kubrick :)

As for the black crosses, technically they were on a transparency placed in front of the film gate and not etched into the lenses. Its possible that some of the transparency could have chipped away on launch, or rubbed off onto the film or leader. The claim is that they sometimes appear behind objects.... first of all, pics or it didn't happen. Second, it makes no logical sense since that would imply that said crosses where on the "movie set" rather than in the camera. Why would you places crosses everywhere if you were trying to fake a moon landing in 1968? We do it now with modern computer effects as a way to track real world objects to super impose CGI elements onto video footage... (aka tracking markers) but again, the computers and software to do that did not exist at the time. And if the crosses where added in a later print, why would they appear behind an object? Again, it makes no sense if you were trying to make a good fake.

And to the point about the cameras being chest mounted, and awkward to use... well yeah they were. In fact, a lot of the really famous photographs had to be cropped and leveled off to the horizon because the framing was so horrible. The cameras they used were modified Hasselblad 500cm medium format cameras. You can actually find the same model on ebay for cheap that still works. The versions they were using actually had no viewfinder, but more of a gun sight apparatus. So you get shots that are horribly canted to the side, or have important objects in a far off corner of the frame. Nasa actually has all of the in-edited still photos and footage on their website, even some of the ones that are over/underexposed, have too much dust on them, or are obstructed by other objects.

So, all I can tell you from a photographic and lighting standpoint, is it looks like what you'd expect the Lunar Surface to look like. In other words, I see nothing to conclusively prove that it was shot using artificial light or trick- photographic effects they had at the time. If they were faked, the images themselves are air-tight.
