Thoughts on Snowden and Catch-22: Snowden is neither hero or traitor. He is a messenger, and his message is "You are being watched", and there's nothing we can do about it.

65  2015-09-21 by wearealllittlealbert

I have been following the Snowden story since the day it broke. At first I thought, like many, that he was a hero. After a while a few things just didn't add up, but this isn't what this post is about. See Naomi Wolfe's article from 2013 to get some idea of what concerns people have had about the Snowden story.

Snowden is neither hero or traitor. If anything, he is merely a messenger, here to tell us that Big Brother is watching us. In 1984, citizens were constantly reminded that they were being watched. In that Orwellian dystopia it was just as important that people were being told constantly that they were being watched as it was that they were being watched.

Snowden fulfills the role of the messenger that constantly reminds us we're being watched. He isn't a genuine whistleblower. He'd be dead by now if he were.

Snowden has an unusual name. In my time, I had never really seen the name before, but there is an interesting literary connection: Snowden was a crucial though marginal character in Joseph Heller's Catch-22. Snowden has a secret which he reveals to Yossarian. Yossarian is attempting to treat Snowden's wound but in the process discovers a fatal wound, which leads to Snowden literally spilling his guts to the floor of the plane.

"Here was God’s plenty, all right, he thought bitterly as he stared — liver, lungs, kidneys, ribs, stomach and bits of the stewed tomatoes Snowden had eaten that day for lunch. "

With Snowden's guts on the floor, Yossarian has a realization:

"Yossarian was cold, too, and shivering uncontrollable. He felt goose pimples clacking all over him as he gazed down despondently at thegrim secret Snowden had spilled all over the messy floor. It was easy to read the message in his entrails. Man was matter, that was Snowden’s secret. Drop him out a window and he’ll fall. Set fire to him and he’ll burn. Bury him and he’ll rot, like other kinds of garbage. The spirit gone, man is garbage. That was Snowden’s secret. Ripeness was all."

See http://www.deathdyinggriefandmourning.com/Death-Dying-Grief-Mourning/100-Joseph-Heller-Catch-22-Death-of-Snowden.htm

So it seems to me that the name "Snowden" was chosen and isn't the real name of the famous whistleblower. The name was meant to evoke the character from Catch-22, who is already associated with "spilling his guts" and in the process revealing a message about our humanity.

But why choose a character from Catch-22 at all? I think they key to that question lies in the logic of a catch-22.

From Wikipedia:

"Catch-22s often result from rules, regulations, or procedures that an individual is subject to but has no control over because to fight the rule is to accept it. Another example is a situation in which someone is in need of something that can only be had by not being in need of it. One connotation of the term is that the creators of the "catch-22" have created arbitrary rules in order to justify and conceal their own abuse of power."

So are the so-called "Snowden revelations" themselves actually a catch-22? It seems clear that as a result, we are constantly aware that we are being watched, but we are powerless to do anything about it. We have no control over what is described as pervasive government surveillance and we can't even fight it. It seems that Snowden, who is rarely out of the limelight, is constantly telling us we are being watched, while also making us aware of how powerless we are against it.

In considering all of this, I see Snowden as possibly serving two objectives:

  • Making all of us aware of the omniscience of the government. The government knows and sees all and Snowden is reminding us of this just about every week.

  • Creating a sense of powerlessness in people. We are being watched, but can do nothing to stop it.

I realize for many here, Snowden is a hero because he confirmed what many of us had suspected all along, but this doesn't mean we should just accept what he is at face value. The truth is, as I discovered when I read Robert McChesney's Digital Disconnect, is that much of what was initially revealed by Snowden was well known. McChesney's book was written prior to Snowden's sudden appearance on the scene, and it covers a great deal of what was known about NSA surveillance. Snowden threw in some extras to keep us interested, but much of it wasn't new.

Whether you believe Snowden is the real deal or not, I think it's clear that he hasn't really provided us with any substantial intelligence that is truly damaging to the United States. In that respect, I don't see him as a traitor. But he isn't a hero either, just a single face we can now put to the government's message to us all: "You are being watched".
